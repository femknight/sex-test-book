The Book

The question they couldn’t answer

Berlin, 2009. Caster Semenya wins the 800m by 8 seconds. Before the result is confirmed, officials order a “battery of tests” to determine her “true sex.”

It is a question that sports institutions have been asking — and failing to definitively answer — since 1967. Sex Test tells the story of how they tried.

Drawing on original archival research in the IOC’s restricted library in Lausanne, Switzerland — including seventeen sets of internal meeting minutes never before analyzed by researchers — this book reconstructs the birth of Olympic gender testing from the inside.

It is a story about science being deployed to do what science cannot do: draw a clean biological line between men and women. And about who pays the price when institutions pretend otherwise.

The Commission acknowledged, in writing, that what it was trying to measure could not be measured. And then proceeded to measure it anyway.

Original Research

The Six Discourse Categories

At the heart of this book is an original discovery from the IOC archives: the Medical Commission simultaneously held six contradictory ways of talking about the body. These were never resolved — they were managed. That management is the story.

Group 1 — Discourses on the Body and Sex Difference

01

The Polymorphic Body

The Commission’s documents acknowledge that the body does not fit neatly into two categories. Medical knowledge of the era already showed that sex characteristics exist on a spectrum and that intersex conditions are natural biological phenomena.

The body is variable. The Commission knew this — and proceeded anyway.

02

The Dimorphic Body

Despite acknowledging polymorphism, the Commission simultaneously maintained that the body naturally belongs to one of two fixed sexes. This contradictory assumption is the entire premise of the testing program.

There is a female category worth protecting. But defining it proved impossible.

03

The Abnormal Body

Bodies that did not conform to dimorphic expectations were framed as medical pathologies to be identified, managed, and excluded. This is where intersex athletes landed — not as natural variations, but as problems.

The “abnormal” body is often just a body that doesn’t fit the test’s assumptions.

Group 2 — Discourses on the Relationship Between the Body and the Commission

04

The Body as Scientific Object

The Commission approached athletes’ bodies as something that could and should be known objectively through science. The athletes themselves became instruments — objects of study, measured and categorized without their full consent.

Objectivity is always a position taken by someone — from somewhere.

05

The Body as Abstract Category

The Commission discussed “the body” in generalized, abstract terms — as a category of analysis rather than as individual people. This depersonalization made bureaucratic decision-making feel clean, even when it wasn’t.

Abstraction makes hard decisions easier. It also makes harm invisible.

06

The Body as Ethical Concern

Remarkably, some Commission members expressed genuine discomfort about the testing program. Internal debate about fairness, accuracy, and dignity appears in the very first meeting’s minutes — and was overruled every time.

The ethics were raised. They were noted. They did not stop anything.

About the Author

Emilie Filion-Donato

Emilie Filion-Donato is a sociologist and researcher whose work sits at the intersection of feminist epistemology, the sociology of science, and sports history.

Her Master’s research required a trip to the IOC’s restricted library in Lausanne, Switzerland — where she became the first scholar to systematically analyze the Medical Commission’s internal documents from 1967–1972. The seventeen sets of meeting minutes, correspondences, and medical reports she examined form the empirical core of Sex Test.

She holds an M.Sc. in Sociology from the Université de Montréal, completed under the supervision of Dr. Christopher McAll (UdeM) and Dr. Petra Lucht (TU Berlin). Sex Test is her first book.
